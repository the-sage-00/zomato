Executive Summary
Every time a Zomato rider waits at a restaurant counter, there is a cost to the rider, to the
customer’s experience, and to Zomato’s operational eﬀiciency. The root cause is almost always the
same: the system dispatched the rider too early because it did not know the kitchen was behind
schedule.
Our investigation traced this back to two fundamental failures in how Zomato currently collects
kitchen timing data. First, the “Food Ready” (FOR) button — the primary signal used to mark
when food is prepared — is almost entirely controlled by the merchant, and merchants routinely
press it only after the rider has already arrived.
This is not occasional human error; it is a
systematic, predictable delay averaging 7.09 minutes for biased merchants. Second, the system
has no visibility into a kitchen’s true workload: dine-in tables, Swiggy orders, and walk-in customers
are all completely invisible to Zomato, yet they directly impact how long cooking takes.
KitchenPulse solves both problems at the data layer, before the model ever sees an input.

1.1 The FOR Button Problem: A Signal That Lies
The FOR button was designed to mark the moment food is ready for pickup. In practice, it marks
something else entirely: the moment the merchant notices the rider standing at the counter. This
distinction is not minor. When a merchant presses the button ten minutes after food was actually
plated, every downstream calculation — rider dispatch timing, customer ETA, kitchen performance
metrics — absorbs that false timestamp as truth.
What makes this especially damaging is that Zomato’s KPT model is trained on historical FOR
data. If a restaurant has been systematically pressing FOR late for months, the model learns that
this restaurant simply “takes longer.” The bias is baked in as a feature, not flagged as noise.
In our simulation, 60% of merchants exhibit this behaviour.
Biased merchants show a
median FOR delay of 7.09 minutes beyond actual food readiness — compared to just 0.31
minutes for honest merchants. This is not noise; it is a learnable, correctable pattern.
Figure 1. FOR button press delay relative to rider arrival time, split by merchant type. Honest merchants
(green) cluster tightly around zero delay. Biased merchants (red) show a wide rightward distribution, con-
firming the delay is systematic rather than random noise.
1.2 The Hidden Load Problem: A System That Cannot See the Kitchen
Even if every merchant pressed the FOR button perfectly, the model would still be wrong during
busy periods — because it has no idea how busy the kitchen actually is.
Consider a restaurant on a Saturday evening: twelve dine-in tables are occupied, four Swiggy orders
are in progress, and a Zomato order just came in. Zomato’s system sees one order and estimates
a normal preparation time. In reality, that kitchen is running at full capacity.
Restaurants in the highest hidden load quartile experience rider wait times 2.4× higher
than those in the lowest quartile — a statistically significant effect (r = +0.38, p < 0.01) that
the current system is entirely blind to.

2.1 Layer 1 — Signal Denoiser
The key insight behind the denoiser is that Zomato already has the data needed to detect merchant
bias — it just is not being used for this purpose. Rider GPS coordinates are available in real
time. FOR button press timestamps are logged. By comparing these two signals, the system can
determine, for every FOR event, whether the rider was already at the restaurant when the button
was pressed.
Any FOR event where the rider was present at press time is classified as a Rider-Proximate FOR
(RP-FOR) event. Over time, the system builds a per-merchant bias offset and subtracts it
from future FOR timestamps to recover a corrected estimate of actual food readiness.
For Tier 1 merchants (large chains with POS systems), the FOR button is bypassed entirely.
Point-of-Sale systems log a ticket-cleared timestamp the moment a kitchen order is closed — au-
tomatically, without any human action.

3 Scalability Across 300,000+ Merchants
One of the most common failure modes in data system proposals is designing for the easy case —
large, well-instrumented restaurants — and glossing over the harder majority. Zomato’s merchant
base is overwhelmingly small businesses: roadside stalls, family-run restaurants, and single-location
takeaways with no technical infrastructure whatsoever.
KitchenPulse is designed around this reality from the ground up, using a tiered routing system
that automatically selects the best available signal path for each merchant.

4 Simulation: Methodology and Results
Zomato provided no dataset for this problem statement. Rather than treating this as a limitation,
we used it as an opportunity to build a simulation with a known, verifiable ground truth —
something a real dataset would not provide. By generating both the true preparation times and
the noisy signals simultaneously, we can measure each signal’s accuracy exactly.
4.1 How the Dataset Was Built
The synthetic dataset contains 17,594 orders across 50 restaurants over a 30-day period.
Three statistical properties of the real world were deliberately replicated:
• Realistic merchant behaviour: 60% of merchants are classified as biased, with individ-
ually sampled bias offsets of 8–20 minutes. The remaining 40% are honest, with near-zero
FOR delay.
• Corrupted model baseline: Zomato’s naive KPT estimate absorbs 85% of each merchant’s
historical bias — exactly how a real model would behave after training on noisy labels for
months.
• Realistic hidden load: Dine-in and competitor order volumes are modelled as time-of-day
Gaussian processes, with lunch and dinner peaks producing elevated hidden load.

The 73.5% reduction in extreme wait events is arguably the most important number.
Tail events — the 10-minute waits, cold food, frustrated riders — generate support tickets,
negative ratings, and cancellations at a rate disproportionate to their volume. Eliminating the
tail has outsized impact relative to improving the average.
6.2 Recommended Deployment Sequence
1. Month 1–2: Shadow Mode Validation. Deploy the KLI microservice as a passive ob-
server in two or three high-density urban zones (Delhi, Mumbai, Bengaluru). It computes
KLI values and logs them alongside every order but does not yet influence dispatch. This val-
idates whether the KLI correlates with observed FOR delays in real-world conditions before
any risk is introduced to live operations.
2. Month 3–4: T2/T3 Denoiser Rollout. Enable per-merchant bias correction system-wide
for all non-POS merchants. Zero merchant communication required; no changes to the mer-
chant app. Measure MAE delta against a matched holdout group. Expected improvement:
15–25% MAE reduction for biased merchants.
3. Month 5–6: T1 POS Integration. Engage the top 100 restaurant chain partners for POS
webhook onboarding. These merchants account for a disproportionate share of high-volume
peak orders, making T1 accuracy gains highest-leverage despite representing only 10% of the
merchant count.
4. Month 7+: Full KLI Dispatch Integration. Activate KLI-adjusted KPT as a live input
to the dispatch engine. Run a rigorous A/B test across matched restaurant cohorts for 4–6
weeks. Primary success metrics: rider wait time, order cancellation rate, and ETA prediction
