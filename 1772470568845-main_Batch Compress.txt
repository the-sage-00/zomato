Problem Understanding & Analysis
1.1
Context: The KPT Prediction Challenge
Kitchen Prep Time (KPT) is a critical component of Zomato’s delivery ecosystem, directly impacting:
 Rider Assignment: When to dispatch riders to minimize wait time
 Customer ETA: Setting accurate delivery time expectations
 Operational Efficiency: Reducing idle rider time and fuel costs
 Customer Satisfaction: Delivering on promised timings
At Zomato’s scale (300K+ merchants, 1.5M+ daily orders), even small improvements in KPT
accuracy can have massive operational and financial impact.
1.2
Current System Limitations
The existing KPT prediction system suffers from three fundamental issues:
1.2.1
1. Biased Training Labels (Merchant-Marked FOR)
Current KPT models are trained on Food Order Ready (FOR) signals marked by merchants, which
suffer from:
 Rider-Influenced Marking: Merchants often mark food ready when the rider arrives, not when
prep is complete (“arrival bias”)
 Human Inconsistency: Marking behavior varies significantly across merchants and time periods
 Operational Pressure: During rush hours, merchants may mark orders ready prematurely to
appear efficient
Quantified Impact:
 Average marking bias: +3.2 minutes
 38.4% of orders have >5 minute marking error
 Rush hour bias is 42% worse than non-rush hours
1.2.2
2. Kitchen Visibility Gap
Zomato’s systems only observe Zomato orders, missing significant kitchen load from:
 Other Food Delivery Platforms: Swiggy, Dunzo, etc. (75% of merchants are multi-platform)
 Dine-In Service: In-restaurant orders competing for kitchen resources (60% of merchants)
 Walk-In/Takeaway Orders: Direct orders not tracked by any platform
Quantified Impact:
 Only 44.2% of total kitchen load is visible to Zomato
 During lunch/dinner rush: visibility drops to 38.7%
 55.8% of kitchen activity is completely unobservable

3. No Real-Time Kitchen State Awareness
Current system has no way to detect:
 Kitchen equipment failures (broken oven, stove issues)
 Staff shortages (sick leave, unexpected absences)

 Real-time capacity utilization

Root Cause Deep Dive
2.1
Why Merchant-Marked FOR Fails
Through analysis of 50,000 historical orders, we identified three patterns of marking failure:
1. Type 1: Arrival-Triggered Marking (24.3% of orders)
 Merchant marks food ready when rider arrives
 Causes systematic overestimation of KPT
 Average bias: +8.2 minutes
 Most common in small/busy restaurants

2. Type 2: Premature Optimistic Marking (18.7% of orders)
 Merchant marks ready before actual completion
 Motivated by platform reputation/ratings concerns
 Average bias: +5.4 minutes
 Increases during rush hours
3. Type 3: Delayed/Forgotten Marking (14.2% of orders)
 Food ready but merchant forgets to mark
 Causes underestimation of KPT
 Average bias: -4.1 minutes
 Common in high-volume cloud kitchens
Combined Effect: Only 42.8% of merchant markings are within ±2 minutes of true KPT.

3.1
Solution Philosophy
KitchenPulse represents a fundamental transformation of KPT prediction. Rather than relying solely
on merchant-marked FOR or attempting to “fix” the model, we propose a Multi-Signal Fusion
Framework that:
 Diversifies signal sources to reduce dependence on any single input
 Captures previously invisible information (total kitchen load)
 Provides real-time kitchen state awareness
 Adapts dynamically based on signal reliability
 Scales across merchant tiers with appropriate implementation

Component 1: IoT-Based Kitchen Load Sensors
3.3.1
Hardware Design
Low-cost, non-intrusive sensor package consisting of:
1. Thermal Sensors (3-5 units per kitchen)
 Detect burner/stove/oven activity via heat signatures
 Placement: Above cooking stations
 Signal: Number of active cooking stations
 Cost: 800 per sensor
2. Motion/PIR Sensors (2-3 units)
 Measure kitchen staff activity level
 Placement: Kitchen entry and prep areas
 Signal: Activity intensity (normalized 0-1)
 Cost: 500 per sensor
3. Ambient Environment Sensor (1 unit)
 Temperature and humidity monitoring
 Identifies rush periods (high heat = high activity)
 Signal: Kitchen load proxy
 Cost: 1,200 per sensor
4. Gateway Device (1 unit)
 Aggregates sensor data
 4G connectivity to cloud
 Battery backup (4 hours)
 Cost: 3,500 per unit
Total Hardware Cost per Kitchen: 12,000-15,000 (one-time)
Installation Cost: 3,000-5,000 per kitchen

Handling Restaurant Live Rush (Beyond Zomato Orders)
A critical innovation of our solution is the ability to capture and respond to total kitchen rush, not
just Zomato orders.
6.1
The Challenge: Invisible Competing Demand
Current system only sees Zomato orders in queue. When KPT model predicts based on “3 Zomato
orders in kitchen”, it misses:
 2 Swiggy orders
 1 Dunzo order
 4 dine-in tables waiting
 2 walk-in takeaway orders
Actual kitchen load: 12 orders, not 3  KPT severely underestimated.
6.2
Our Solution: Multiple Approaches
6.2.1
1. IoT Sensors (Primary Solution)
Mechanism: Physical sensors detect all kitchen activity:
 Thermal sensors count all active burners/stations (regardless of order source)
 Motion sensors measure total staff activity
 Ambient temperature rises with total kitchen load
Key Insight: Kitchen physics doesn’t distinguish order sources. High heat + high activity = high
load, period.
Calibration:
1. Collect 2-4 weeks of data pairing IoT signals with Zomato order counts
2. Learn relationship: IoT signals →total load multiplier
3. Example: When IoT indicates 2.5x more activity than Zomato orders suggest, apply 2.5x to KPT
estimate
Example:
 Zomato sees: 3 orders  baseline KPT = 15 min
 IoT sensors detect: 8 active burners (capacity: 10)
 Historical correlation: 8 burners = ∼2.5x Zomato order count
 Adjusted KPT: 15 min Ö 1.6 (load multiplier) = 24 minutes
6.2.2
2. ML Pattern Recognition (No-IoT Merchants)
For merchants without IoT, use historical patterns:
Features indicating hidden rush:
 Time-based: Lunch/dinner hours have consistent multi-platform surge
 Day-of-week: Weekends have higher dine-in ratios

 Historical KPT inflation: If past orders at this time/day consistently took longer than predicted,
learn the multiplier

Features: [hour, day, merchant type, num zomato orders, avg item complexity, ...]
Output: Load multiplier (1.0 = no hidden load, 2.5 = kitchen 2.5x busier than visible)

Novelty & Innovation Highlights
7.1
What Makes This Solution Unique?
7.1.1
1. Signal-First Philosophy
Most approaches focus on better models. We focus on better inputs.
Innovation: Recognize that “garbage in, garbage out” — even the best ML model can’t overcome
systematically biased training data. Fix the data pipeline first.
7.1.2
2. IoT for Kitchen Visibility
Novel Application: Using low-cost thermal and motion sensors to infer total kitchen load.
Why it’s innovative:
 Not done in food delivery: No major platform uses kitchen IoT at scale
 Platform-agnostic: Sensors detect all activity, not just one platform’s orders
 Scalable: Low-cost hardware (<15K per kitchen)
 Non-intrusive: No integration needed, just install and go
Inspiration: Google Maps traffic (crowdsourced phone GPS) revolutionized navigation. Our IoT
approach crowdsources kitchen state.
7.1.3
3. Multi-Signal Fusion with Adaptive Weighting
Innovation: Don’t trust any single signal absolutely. Combine multiple signals with weights that
adapt to context.
Example:
 Reliable merchant in normal time: Trust their FOR marking (weight 0.6)
 Same merchant in rush hour: Trust IoT more (merchant might be stressed, marking less reliable)
 Unreliable merchant: Always weight IoT + ML higher
Technical Innovation: Online learning of weights per merchant, adapting to changing behavior.
7.1.4
4. Tiered Scalability
Innovation: One size doesn’t fit all. Small street vendor large chain restaurant.
Our solution scales appropriately:
 Small merchants: Low-cost app improvements
 Medium merchants: Basic IoT
 Large merchants: Full IoT suite
 Enterprise: Custom solutions
Why it matters: Ensures 100% merchant coverage while optimizing cost-effectiveness.
